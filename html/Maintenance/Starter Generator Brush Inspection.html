This section provides instructions for cleaning the starter generator and inspecting the brushes. These instructions have been historically published in Service Letter 212 and are now provided in this section for convenience.

Skurka Aerospace also publishes several documents on this topic to assist with the maintenance of their starter generators. In particular, one should reference:

  • Service Letter No. 193 - Brush Life Calculations at First Inspection of the Starter - Generator (300SG & 300SGL Series Starter-Generators)
  • Service Letter No. 178 - Data Which Will Allow You to Calculate Your Brush Life for Your SAI Starter - Generator (200SG & 250SG Series Starter-Generators)

Brush and commutator wear is a function of electrical load, environmental conditions, and brush grade. Brush performance is subject to aircraft operation methods (i.e. short flights vs. long flights). Short flights, with a high number of starts, degrade brushes quickly and reduces brush life. During operations, individual brush wear rates may vary significantly dependent upon selectivity (the tendency for a higher percentage of total current flow to travel through one brush), heavy electrical loads, brush type, and variations in brush block spring tension.

For these reasons, Air Tractor recommends periodic brush checks . Along with the brush check, a thorough inspection and cleaning of the starter generator should be accomplished. The following is provided as a general guide to perform the inspection.

Procedure

  1. Remove starter generator.
  2. Inspect for overall condition and signs of overheating. Visually inspect for damage to components such as fan, shroud, shaft, and main terminals.
  3. Stand unit vertically and remove brush cover. Mark brushes and holders to ensure brushes can be returned to the original holder, or remove brushes one at a time to inspect for wear and service life remaining. A properly seated brush will have uniform wear and display a smooth / semi-gloss surface. A minimum of 75% in the axial direction and 100% in the rotational direction is recommended.
  4. Remove carbon dust using compressed air. Starting at the shaft (drive) end, working your way toward the fan (anti-drive) end.
  5. With brushes removed, inspect commutator appearance. If commutator is grooved, it is recommended that the commutator be resurfaced by a qualified repair station prior to a brush change. If commutator is damaged, it is recommended that the unit be returned to a qualified repair station for service.
  6. Rotate the unit slowly in the direction of normal rotation. Normal rotation can be found on the I.D. plate and is interpreted with the splined shaft facing you. The bearings should rotate relatively smooth with no evident binding. If damage is suspected, it is recommended that the unit be returned to a qualified repair station for service.
  7. Using an Ohmmeter, check the insulation resistance between Positive terminal B(+) and the housing. Resistance measurement should be 10K Ohms or greater. If resistance measurement is less than 10K Ohms, clean the generator with compressed air again and retest. If the generator does not pass the electrical resistance check, it is recommended that the unit be returned to a qualified repair station for service.
  8. Inspect brush lead formation to ensure that the brushes can travel freely through the holder with brush wear. If a brush “hangs up” in the holder, it may lose contact with the commutator causing the generator to fail.
  9. If a brush change is required, consult the appropriate starter generator manufacturer instructions for brush installation, seating, lead formation, and run-in.
